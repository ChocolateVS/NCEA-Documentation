Relationships between Roots and Coefficients

If \( \alpha \) and, \( \beta \)

are the roots of the quadratic equation:

\( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \)


Then:

\( \alpha + \beta = (-b)/a \)


\( \alpha\beta = c/a \)


Proof:

\( (x - \alpha)(x - \beta)= x^2 + {b \over a}x + {c \over a} \)


\( x^2 - (\alpha + \beta)x + \alpha\beta = x^2 + {b \over a}x + {c \over a} \), Simplified


Equate the co-efficients on both sides:

\( -(\alpha + \beta) = {b \over a} \), Co-efficients of x


\( \alpha\beta = {c \over a} \), Constant Numbers



Suppose that \( \alpha, \beta, and \gamma \), are the roots of a cubic equation of the type \( ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0\).


By factor theorem, since

\( x = \alpha\) is a root of the cubic equation \( (x - \alpha)\) must be a factor and, in a similar way, so must \( (x - \beta) \) and \( (x - \gamma) \).


Therefore:

\( (x - \alpha)(x - \beta)(x - \gamma) = 0 \)


By Expanding the brackets and comparing the result with \( ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0 \), the following results are obtained:


\( \alpha + \beta + \gamma = {(-b) \over a}\)


\( \alpha\beta\gamma = {(-d) \over a}\)
